EV vs Gas: The Real Cost of Ownership

By EV Time Team · 5 min read

Everyone talks about sticker price. But that’s the wrong number to fixate on. The real question is: what does this car cost to own over 5 years? That’s where EVs often start to pull ahead.

The Sticker Price Myth

Yes, EVs can cost more upfront, especially when you compare trims and features apples-to-apples. But the upfront gap doesn’t tell the full story — because fuel and maintenance are where a lot of the money gets spent.

Quick note: Real costs vary by electricity rate, gas price, annual miles, tire wear, insurance, incentives, and the model you choose. The numbers below use realistic assumptions and show ranges so the math stays honest over time.

Fuel Costs: Usually the Biggest Swing

The average American drives about 13,500 miles per year. EV energy cost depends mostly on your home electricity rate (and whether you use off-peak pricing). Gas cost depends on local fuel prices and MPG.

Category (Typical)EVGas Car
Annual fuel/energy cost$600–$1,100$1,700–$2,800
5-year fuel cost$3,000–$5,500$8,500–$14,000
Savings over 5 yearsOften $4,000–$8,500 saved with EV

In plain English: if you mostly charge at home, EVs are usually cheaper to “fuel.” If you rely heavily on public fast charging, savings can shrink — but EVs often still come out ahead vs typical gas costs.

Maintenance: The Quiet Advantage

Gas cars have more routine service items. EVs are mechanically simpler — fewer fluids, fewer wear items, and fewer engine-related repairs.

  • Oil changes: $0 (EVs don’t have engine oil)
  • Brake pads: Often last longer thanks to regenerative braking
  • Exhaust system: Not applicable to EVs
  • Typical maintenance: EVs often spend less over time, but tires can wear faster on some models

Over 5 years, many drivers see meaningful maintenance savings with an EV — often thousands, depending on the vehicle and how it’s driven.

Tax Credits and Incentives

Incentives can help a lot — but they’re not automatic. Eligibility varies by vehicle, purchase type (new vs used), buyer income, and location. Some buyers may qualify for federal and state incentives; others won’t. Always verify your specific situation before counting on a credit.

Bottom line: When you factor in fuel savings + lower routine maintenance (and incentives when eligible), EVs often match or beat comparable gas cars on 5-year ownership cost — even if the EV costs more upfront.

What About Depreciation?

Depreciation is model-specific and changes with market conditions. Some EVs (and some gas cars) hold value extremely well. Others drop faster. The right move is to evaluate resale value for the exact model/trim/year you’re considering — not assume EVs always win or always lose here.

The Verdict

Sticker price is a distraction. When you zoom out and look at total cost of ownership, EVs often come out cheaper for many drivers — especially if you charge at home and drive average-to-high miles per year.

The question isn’t just whether you can afford an EV upfront. It’s whether the 5-year math works in your favor.
