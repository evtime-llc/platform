EV vs Gas: The Real Cost of Ownership

By EV Time Team · 5 min read

Everyone talks about sticker price. But that’s the wrong number to fixate on. The real question is: what does this car cost you over 5 years? That’s where EVs often start to pull ahead.

The Sticker Price Myth

Yes, EVs can cost more upfront. But upfront price doesn’t tell the whole story because a large part of ownership cost happens after you buy the car: energy, maintenance, insurance, and depreciation.

Simple rule: If you want the honest comparison, use total cost of ownership (TCO): Purchase + Energy + Maintenance + Insurance + Depreciation (minus any incentives you actually qualify for).

Energy Cost: Where EVs Often Win

Most EV owners save money because electricity per mile is typically cheaper than gasoline per mile — especially when charging at home. Your results depend mainly on:

  • Your electricity rate (and whether you have off-peak pricing)
  • Your gas price and MPG
  • How many miles you drive per year
  • How much you rely on public fast charging
CategoryEV (Typical)Gas Car (Typical)

Bottom line: if you can charge at home most of the time, the math usually favors an EV. If you rely heavily on fast charging, savings can shrink — but EVs can still compete depending on gas prices and efficiency.

Maintenance: The Quiet Advantage

EVs are mechanically simpler. That usually means fewer routine service items compared to gas cars. Common differences:

  • No oil changes
  • No exhaust system
  • Fewer fluids and fewer engine-related repairs
  • Brake wear can be lower due to regenerative braking
  • Tires can wear faster on some EVs because of weight and torque

Insurance: Model-Dependent

Insurance varies a lot by driver, city, repair costs, and vehicle model. Some EVs insure similarly to gas cars; others can cost more. Always quote insurance on the exact model/trim you’re considering.

Depreciation: Don’t Assume Either Side Wins

Depreciation is where many people get surprised — and it changes with market conditions. Some EVs hold value extremely well. Some don’t. Same for gas cars. The only reliable way to judge is to check resale trends for the exact model year and trim you’re buying.

Bottom line: If you charge mostly at home and drive average-to-high miles, EVs often win on 5-year ownership cost. If you rarely drive, can’t charge at home, or insurance is much higher, the advantage can shrink.

The Verdict

Sticker price is a distraction. A fair comparison looks at total cost of ownership. For many drivers, EVs already make financial sense over 5 years — and the more you drive (with home charging), the stronger the case often becomes.

The question isn’t just “Can I afford an EV?” It’s “Does the 5-year math work for my situation?”
