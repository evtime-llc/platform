How to Buy a Used EV (What to Check)

By EV Time Team · 6 min read

Buying a used EV is one of the smartest moves you can make right now. Prices have dropped, technology is proven, and the savings are real. But there are things you need to check that don't apply to gas cars. Here's your complete guide.

1. Battery Health Is Everything

The battery is the most expensive component in any EV — replacing one can cost $8,000 to $20,000+. The good news: most EV batteries last far longer than people think. But you still need to verify.

  • Ask for a battery health report. Tesla shows degradation in the app. Other brands use third-party tools like Recurrent Auto.
  • Check the range vs original. If a car was rated for 310 miles new and now shows 270 at full charge, that's about 87% health — totally normal for a 3-4 year old car.
  • Below 80% is a red flag. Most warranties guarantee 70-80% over 8 years/100K miles. If it's already below 80%, negotiate hard or walk away.

Pro tip: A battery at 90% health after 50,000 miles is excellent. Degradation slows down over time — most of the loss happens in the first 2 years, then levels off.

2. Check the Warranty Transfer

Most EV manufacturers offer 8-year/100,000-mile battery warranties, and these typically transfer to the second owner. But verify:

  • Is the battery warranty still active? Check miles and date.
  • Does it transfer? Tesla, Hyundai, Kia, Ford — yes. Some brands have restrictions.
  • Is the bumper-to-bumper warranty still active? Usually 3-4 years/50K miles. Probably expired on most used EVs, which is fine.

3. Software and Updates

Unlike gas cars, EVs get better over time through software updates. But this varies wildly by brand.

  • Tesla: Over-the-air updates continuously. A 2021 Model 3 has better software today than when it was new.
  • Other brands: Some offer OTA updates, some require dealer visits. Ask whether the car is on the latest software version.
  • Watch for deactivated features. Some features (like Tesla's FSD or Enhanced Autopilot) are tied to the owner's account, not the car. Confirm what transfers with the sale.

4. Charging Setup

Before you buy, figure out your charging situation:

  • Home charging: Do you have a garage or dedicated parking spot? A standard outlet (Level 1) adds ~4 miles/hour. A 240V outlet (Level 2) adds ~25-30 miles/hour.
  • Does the car come with a charger? Tesla includes a mobile connector. Some brands don't include one — that's an extra $300-500.
  • What charging network does it use? Tesla Supercharger network is the largest. Most other EVs now have adapters for it too.

5. Tire Condition

EVs are heavier than gas cars and have instant torque. This means tires wear faster. Check:

  • Tread depth. EV tires may need replacement every 25,000-30,000 miles vs 40,000+ for gas cars.
  • Are they EV-specific tires? EVs perform best with low rolling resistance tires designed for their weight and torque.
  • Budget $800-$1,200 for a set. Factor this into your purchase if the tires are worn.

6. Accident and Service History

Same rules as any used car, but with some EV-specific checks:

  • Run a VIN check (Carfax, AutoCheck) for accidents and title status.
  • Undercarriage damage matters more. The battery pack sits under the car. Any major underside impact is a concern.
  • Service records. EVs need less maintenance, but check that brake fluid, cabin filters, and coolant were serviced on schedule.

Your Used EV Checklist

  • ✅ Battery health report (aim for 85%+ for cars under 5 years)
  • ✅ Warranty status and transferability
  • ✅ Software version — is it current?
  • ✅ Included features — what transfers with the car?
  • ✅ Charging cable included?
  • ✅ Tire condition and type
  • ✅ VIN check — clean title, no major accidents
  • ✅ Undercarriage inspection
  • ✅ Test drive — regen braking feel, range estimate accuracy
  • ✅ Home charging plan figured out

The Bottom Line

Used EVs are an incredible value right now. Depreciation has done the hard work for you — you can get a car that cost $50,000 new for $25,000-$30,000, with most of its battery life still ahead of it.

Just do your homework on the battery, warranty, and features. If those check out, you're getting one of the best deals in the car market today.
